by Minh Cuong Hoang, Maria Nankova, Shourya Bhattacharya, and Nura Kawa

It goes without saying that 2020 has been characteristically marked by the Covid-19 pandemic. One of the primary tools that policy makers across the world have chosen to curb the spread of Covid-19 is to restrict public mobility. Consequently, most nations experienced economic decline. Over time, such regulations have been repeatedly enforced and relaxed in response to existing numbers of Covid cases. The objective of this project is to visually explore how mobility has changed throughout 2020. Additionally, we look for patters that reflect the effect of changes in mobility on the economy of individual nations.

Data

Since the beginning of the Covid-19 pandemic, Google has provided open-source Mobility Reports. From Google: "These Community Mobility Reports aim to provide insights into what has changed in response to policies aimed at combating COVID-19. The reports chart movement trends over time by geography, across different categories of places such as retail and recreation, groceries and pharmacies, parks, transit stations, workplaces, and residential." From February 6, 2020, the data show for each day how visits and length of stay at different places change compared to a baseline. A Global View

We begin with the following question:
Throughout 2020, which nation saw the highest change in mobility?

Below, the interactive globe shows the nation with the largest absolute change in mobility from May 1 to October 1, 2020. Notice that, in particular, South and Central American countries see a change in mobility sequentially in the middle of the year.

To interact with the globe, click on a country name in the list!


Intuitively, we would think that the nation with the largest change in mobility at a time point also experienced the largest peak in covid cases or deaths. Could that be the case?

Below, you can see which continent has the highest incidence of covid cases or deaths over each week in 2020. Hover your cursor over a bar to see the value. NOTE: This is square-root scaling for better visibility.

There seems to be some relationship, but not strong. Looking at Covid-19 deaths per million, South America dominates the middle of 2020, while Europe dominates the beginning and end of the year. Europe and North America have peaks that subside; South America maintains a rather consistent wave.

Indeed, GDP has decreased in nearly all countries. Additionally, South America, Europe and North America appear to have had the highest number of Covid-19 deaths per million. With Europe suffering heavily in numbers of Covid and facing a slowing economy, let's examine mobility in Europe more closely.

A European View

While, in the global view, Europe appears similar as a whole, mobility varies between countries. In the plot below, we can compare daily change in mobility between three countries for any number of days in 2020. There are a lot of insights that can be gathered with this visualization tool.

For instance, if we select France, Sweden and United Kingdom for the month of May, we notice that France has the most decrease in mobility, while Sweden sees an increase in mobility in most dimensions. French increase their mobility to grocery and pharmacy, while Swedish go more to parks. French and British increase their time at home, while the Swedish have nearly zero net change.

Select three distinct countries, select a time frame, click "filter"!


Let's see how covid cases might play a role in this. In the scatterplot below, we plot the monthly average number of new cases per million against the net change in mobility (average over all indicators, average over 1 month).

Start by selecting a month. Hover the mouse over the scatterplot. It should look like a + sign. Then, drag and select the bubbles of interest. A chart will appear! To remove it, drag and select some empty space. To change month, click a button!
It's interesting to look at the change in pattern over time. First, it seems that the net change in mobility is not strongly correlated with covid in Europe - this is similar to what we observed in the global view. In February, the outlying country is Italy - it had by far the highest number of cases per million. In May, the outlier is Sweden - a nation that famously did not enforce a national lockdown. Mobility hardly changed from baseline. As time goes on, neighboring countries tend to form clusters.

Country-Level View

To get insight into individual nations, let's look at daily mobility within a country. We can see below, for each nation and each mobility indicator, how mobility has changed over time. We also see a linear calendar showing the beginning and end of covid measures.

If we look at France, there appears to be a strong correlation between mobility and measures, especially residential and workplace mobility. The measures start and stop at roughly the same time, making the connection easy to see. Other countries stagger their measures - for example, Italy had many measures from the beginning. Driving in particular seems to increase when there are fewer measures in place and decrease when there are many measures in place. Finally, Sweden is an interesting example. Only two measures were in place in all of 2020. However, we do see some patterns in mobility that were seen by other nations. For instance, a relative decrease in workplace mobility on weekends, and an increase of mobility in parks in the summer. Most nations have these patterns, as well as sharp changes on national holidays (for instance, look at July 14 in France).
